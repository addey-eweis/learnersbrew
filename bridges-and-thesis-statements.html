Thesis Statements

The thesis statement is the most important part of an essay or a research paper. It's where you get to summarizing your argument, backing it up with evidence and examples throughout the essay, and explaining your position on an issue. In short, it's what your writing is all about.

A thesis statement is usually the first sentence in an essay or the first few sentences in a research paper. It states your opinion on a topic, and it informs your readers of the direction that you'll be heading with the rest of your words. It's what ties everything together, so to speak. Taking out or muddying up your thesis statement can leave you with serious problems later on in your writing process.

What makes a good thesis statement? Effective thesis statements are clear and concise. A well-constructed sentence should be able to catch readers' attention right away.

In any case, a good thesis will always sound like this: "This is what I'm arguing in my paper/essay. Here's what I think about this subject. Here's what I'm trying to prove." It's up to you to back up that opinion with evidence and to explain your position in more detail.

The thesis statement should be a declarative sentence (i.e., a statement of fact, not a question) so that you know exactly where you stand from the very beginning. In order for the body of your paper or essay to follow through on what your thesis statement promises, you need to provide a thorough explanation using examples and evidence. This kind of clarity will make it easier for readers to understand exactly where you're coming from and evaluate whether they agree or disagree with your reasoning and evidence.

There's a lot of room for creativity when it comes to the structure of your thesis statement, but it should always contain the essential information. One trick that students often use is to replace the subject and verb in their thesis statement with a question. For instance, "This paper questions how effective telecommuting really is" becomes "Is telecommuting effective?" By turning your thesis statement into a question, you prompt your readers to reflect on what you're about to say. It also leaves open the possibility that they'll come up with an answer that you didn't consider before and thus have something new to think about (and write about) in your paper or essay.
