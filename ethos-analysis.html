Ethos

In this brief article, I will explain the definition of ethos and how it is used by various scholars to analyze literature and other cultural products.

Ethos is a term in rhetoric that refers to persuasive elements such as language, tone, character emotions and even action. As a rhetorical device it can be used in almost any discourse form including fiction. In non-fiction especially, ethos helps suss out the real intentions of the author or speaker behind their claims. The idea behind ethos is to give the audience an easy way to see what the author or speaker is actually like based on their writing. It helps communicate a point effectively and allows for a better understanding of the text.

As a rhetorical device, ethos is used primarily in persuasive arguments and includes those that can be categorized as logos (the truth/logic of argument) and pathos (emotion/persuasion). Ethos in fiction relies heavily on pathos because it adds depth to the narrative. In this way, ethos can be seen as a means of persuasion and emotion.

Ethos is also one of the four ancient elements. The other three are logos (the truth/logic of argument), pathos (emotion/persuasion) and chreia (persuasion through rhetoric). These four elements are described in Aristotle’s Rhetoric. They were later developed in Aristotle’s Poetics, a book primarily focused on how to analyze tragedies. Since that time, these elements have largely been accepted as a basic understanding of persuasion and emotional appeal in any piece of writing.

As a rhetorical theory, ethos is a powerful tool in analyzing complex texts. It helps readers understand the world through the author’s perspective. It assists the audience in identifying ethos by discussing various examples from literature and other cultural products. At the same time, it identifies how each author uses ethos as they develop their argument or viewpoint, which allows readers to see what differentiates one piece of writing from another.
