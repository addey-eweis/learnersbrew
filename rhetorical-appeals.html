Rhetorical Appeals

Rhetorical appeals are the ways people influence a reader to feel or act a certain way without providing logical arguments or other evidence in support. Sometimes called "persuasive appeals" or "rhetorical moves," they rely on emotional characteristics, such as surprise, humor, sadness or anger, that challenge the audience to rethink their opinions.

Pathos

Pathos is the quality of arousing feelings of pity or fear in an audience by describing a situation that seems hopeless. It is both inspiring and motivating to people in distress.

Ethos

Ethos is a term in rhetoric that refers to persuasive elements such as language, tone, character emotions and even action. As a rhetorical device it can be used in almost any discourse form including fiction. In non-fiction especially, ethos helps suss out the real intentions of the author or speaker behind their claims.

Logos

Logos is one of three appeals in rhetoric, logos, pathos, and ethos. Logos is the appeal to logic and reason; pathos is the appeal to the emotions; and ethos is the appeal to credibility. It's a persuasive appeal to audience's logic.

Rhetorical Appeals The ad is using Ethos to hook the reader and establish a sense of trust, it is used to recommend the product to the reader, It’s using a doctor’s quote to praise the product and making the reader think that it’s worth trying.

 Showing the reader a picture of a doctor is a form of Pathos, it makes the reader feel that it’s more credible than just putting the quote without the picture. A picture of a human immediately makes something appear more real, so this definitely forms an emotional attachment to the reader.

 The ad is using Logos to show the reader some stats about the product, and this provides the reader credibility to further pursued the reader buy, it markets the product as the best toothpaste for sensitive teeth, plus it’s recommended by dentists.
